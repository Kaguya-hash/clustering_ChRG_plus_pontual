ChRG+

This is the ChRG+ information page.

The Problem with Traditional Clustering

Traditional clustering methods, like K-means, often struggle because they reduce a cluster to a single representative point. This makes it difficult to capture more complex geometric properties in the data. While some methods try to fix this using multiple points, they often rely on heuristics and lack a unified theoretical framework.

The ChRG+ Approach

To address this problem, the ChRG+ method treats cluster representation as an explicit and central geometric structure.

It introduces the concept of an abstract multi-geometric representation, designed to accurately capture the intrinsic shape of a cluster, including complex and heterogeneous forms. This representation is constructed through the novel LDg method (Generalized Discrete Lloyd Method), which exhibits important properties such as convergence toward a local minimum — except in rare pathological cases involving strong hiper-symmetries in the data — and, in certain scenarios, stability under random variations in the dataset.

These representations are integrated directly into a hierarchical clustering process, ensuring that the geometric structure of the data itself guides the formation of clusters.

Resistance to Noise

To deal with noise, ChRG+ also incorporates an explicit strategy to resist outliers by penalizing merges that involve very small clusters. This prevents isolated points from distorting the main clusters.

Results and Evaluation

The method was evaluated using standard benchmark datasets from the scikit-learn library. The results showed a strong ability to capture complex structures and maintain consistency while handling outliers.

Limitations

While effective, the current study identified several limitations:

  • Dataset Scope: Experiments were primarily conducted on two-dimensional datasets with relatively small sizes, typically between 500 and 1000 points.
  • Hyperparameter Dependency: The method relies on the selection of specific parameters—the number of expected clusters (K), the number of representative geometries (S), and the parameters for outlier penalization (M and lambda). In this framework, K, S, and M are natural numbers, while lambda is a value greater than or equal to zero. Currently, these parameters lack a systematic or automated selection strategy and were chosen based on a priori knowledge.
  • Representative Types: The initial evaluation focused largely on point-based representatives rather than more complex geometric structures.

Future Work

Future exploration for the ChRG+ method will focus on:

  • Theoretical Foundations: Developing systematic and theoretically grounded strategies for the selection of hyperparameters.
  • Complex Representatives: Testing and experimenting with non-point representatives to explore their impact on the quality of data partitions.
  • Scalability: Analyzing the behavior and performance of the method in higher-dimensional spaces and with much larger datasets (higher cardinality) to address computational costs.
